About Us

LIT Review is a group of early career scientists and physicians-in-training committed to the art of scientific communication and presentation. We believe that as the next generation's scientists, physicians, and leaders, we have a responsibility to communicate our science in a manner that is clear, concise, and actionable. We believe training in scientific communication should begin as early as possible; moreover, with recent societal shifts towards remote communication, our scientific presentations need to be more polished and unambiguous than ever. In LIT Review, we broadly review literature within a field (of which we claim no expertise), present high-level overviews of select topics, and solicit discourse from our peers. Please tune in and join us on our journey towards communicative excellence!

Meet the Team

Danica Dy

Hi there, welcome to LIT Review! My name is Danica Dy and I am a recent graduate (Class of 2020) from UNC-Chapel Hill. I received my BS in Biology with minors in Chemistry and Medicine, Literature, & Culture. I am an incoming PhD student for UNC’s Biological and Biomedical Sciences Program and am taking the next year of rotations to decide on my dedicated field of study.

I started my research journey with summers in the Gilbride Lab at Ryerson University, studying antibiotic resistance in bacteria from aquatic environments. We were interested in studying the dissemination of resistance genes through waterways in urban and rural settings. I also spent a significant part of my undergraduate degree conducting research in Cell Biology and Physiology with the Caron Lab at UNC-Chapel Hill. I worked on projects that studied the Adrenomedullin-signaling axis on angiogenesis in mouse models, and looked at lymphangiogenesis phenotypes in Collaborative Cross mouse models. These biological research projects led me to question the broader implications of these research findings on global public health.

Could understanding the dissemination of resistant genes through our waterways influence how we treat our water and what communities are at higher risk? Could differing levels of hormones across sexes or genetic diversity influence the signaling axis for lymphangiogenesis and thus affect risk for associated diseases and therapeutic development? Therefore, I am incredibly interested in applying biological and biomedical principles in the broader context of improving global public health. I hope that whichever field and career I decide on, I will be able to make a positive impact on human health and advance the understanding of pathophysiology so that we can provide advanced therapeutics and share information about disease prevention and mitigation through stronger health systems.

Timothy Qi

I graduated from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill with a B.S. in Biology and B.A. in Chemistry and am pursuing a career in clinical, quantitative, and systems pharmacology. In particular, I hope to innovate upon Model-Informed Drug Development paradigms such that the mechanisms, PK/PD, and clinical relevance of complex therapeutic platforms can be confidently evaluated across all stages of development. Through understanding these factors a priori, I endeavor to accelerate the translation of novel therapeutics from bench to bedside. Auxiliary to this process is the furthering of widespread acceptance of modeling and simulation by clinical and regulatory decision makers, which necessitates demystification of methodology and clear scientific communication. To this end, I founded LIT Review to facilitate the development of scientific communication skills.

Ryesa Amreen Mansoor

Hi everyone! My name is Ryesa. I’m a recent graduate from the UNC Chapel Hill Gillings School of Public Health, where I received my BSPH in Nutrition and a double minor in Neuroscience and Korean. I am currently a Post-baccalaureate Research Fellow at the NIMH working under the Section for Functional Imaging Methods. Building off of my past research experience, during my four years of undergrad I worked in two different neuroimaging-focused labs. My first lab was a cognitive neuroimaging lab where I worked on attention, learning, and cognition experiments using EEG and fMRI analysis. My most recent lab studied the neuropsychology of ingestive behaviors, where I worked on projects using fMRI to evaluate brain changes and learning in response to certain eating behaviors. I’m very interested in research that encompasses the intersectionality of various disciplines - like nutrition and neuroscience - that allow us to understand the microscopic and macroscopic bases of human behavior and health. After completing my 2 years at the NIH, I plan to attend medical school and receive a dual MD/MPH degree. I hope to continue studying human health and behavior from a public health perspective and apply this to patient care as a physician in the future.
